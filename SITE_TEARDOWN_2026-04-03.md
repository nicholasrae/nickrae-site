# nickrae.net Teardown — Conversion & Credibility Review
**Date:** 2026-04-03
**Reviewed by:** Hermes
**Pages reviewed:** Homepage, Kalshi weather bot post, OpenClaw Playbook post, blog index, supporting posts

---

## 1. Positioning Clarity

### What is immediately clear:
- You are a commercial pilot funding flight hours through side projects.
  That narrative is strong and present on every page. It gives people a
  reason to root for you.
- You build real things. The project list is concrete, not aspirational.
- You are not a developer. This is actually a selling point for the
  Playbook and it comes through.

### What is vague:
- **Who this site is for.** The homepage serves at least four audiences
  simultaneously: airlines hiring pilots, people who might buy apps,
  people who might buy the Playbook, and people who might buy study
  guides. None of them get a clear path.
- **"Pilot · Builder · Maker"** is a tagline that describes you but tells
  a visitor nothing about what they get. Everyone is a maker now.
  This is a self-description, not a value proposition.
- **FeedFare's one-liner is confusing.** "Walk to unlock your apps" is
  clever but unclear. A first-time reader doesn't know if it's parental
  controls, a fitness app, or screen time management. It's all three,
  and that's the problem.

### What is trying to do too many jobs:
- **The homepage is a personal portfolio, a product catalog, a flight
  resume, and a blog landing page.** It lists 9 projects, 7 books, flight
  credentials, work history, education, and 6 contact channels. A hiring
  manager, a potential Playbook buyer, and a student pilot all land on
  the same page and get the same wall of everything.
- **The "What I'm Building" section has no hierarchy.** FeedFare (your
  primary focus), the Playbook ($99 product), word search books, and a
  nonprofit are all given equal visual weight. The $99 Playbook is item
  #8 of 9. The word search books are above it.

## 2. CTA Strength

### Homepage
- **What should a visitor do?** Unclear. There are 4 hero buttons, 10+
  Amazon links, links to Gumroad, Payhip, ClawHub, the App Store, and
  GitHub. The paradox of choice is extreme.
- The strongest revenue CTA ("Get the Playbook — $99") is buried as
  project #8 in a list of 9. A visitor who doesn't scroll past the
  word search books will never see it.
- "Flight Funded Tracker" is in the hero but generates zero revenue.
  It's interesting content but shouldn't compete with money-making CTAs
  for hero placement.

### Kalshi Weather Bot Post
- **CTAs at the bottom:** Flight Funded tracker, PPL Workbook, IR
  Workbook. None of these are the natural next step for someone who
  just read about your trading bot. The natural CTA is the OpenClaw
  Playbook, which contains the bot's setup guide. It's not linked.
- The post is strong content with real numbers and real honesty. It
  builds credibility well. But it doesn't convert that credibility
  into anything. Someone reads it, thinks "this guy is legit," and
  has nowhere to go.

### OpenClaw Playbook Post
- **This is the best page on the site.** The stats bar (47 crons, $30/mo
  cost, $352 revenue, 0 lines of code) is excellent. The "who this is
  for" section is clear. The discount code (FIRSTFLIGHT, 30% off) gives
  urgency.
- **Problem:** The CTA appears once at the bottom. On a 2,000+ word page,
  there should be at least two: one after the stats bar / "what the
  playbook covers" section (for people who already know they want it)
  and one at the bottom (for people who read the whole thing).
- The price ($99) is stated only in the CTA link text. It should appear
  earlier, near the stats, so visitors can evaluate whether to keep
  reading.

## 3. Trust Signals

### What works:
- **Real numbers everywhere.** $1,773 paper profit, 76.5% WR, 369.5
  flight hours, 57.9% live WR, $279 live P&L. You don't hide the
  mediocre numbers (live WR lag, the $340 API mistake, the BOUNDARY_FADE
  -$251 disaster). This is rare and builds real trust.
- **"What I Got Wrong" sections** in the blog posts. Most people only
  share wins. You document failures with specific dollar amounts. This
  is your strongest trust signal.
- **Verifiable credentials.** Commercial pilot certificates, Amazon book
  listings with reviews, App Store links. These are checkable.
- **The $340 mistake story** in the Playbook post is worth more than any
  testimonial. It proves you actually use what you're selling.

### What's missing:
- **Zero testimonials or social proof for the Playbook.** No buyer
  count, no reviews, no quotes, no "X people have bought this." Even
  "12 copies sold" would help.
- **No screenshots of the Playbook interior.** You're asking for $99
  for a PDF. Show 2-3 pages. Show the SOUL.md template, the cron
  job config, the bot architecture diagram. Let people see what
  they're buying.
- **No live P&L screenshot or dashboard embed.** The Kalshi post talks
  about results but shows no visuals. A screenshot of the trading
  dashboard or a simple P&L chart would make the numbers feel real
  instead of claimed.
- **Amazon reviews are mentioned nowhere.** Your PPL Workbook has 5.0
  stars and 4 reviews. That should be on the Aviation Resources
  section. "★★★★★ 5.0 (4 reviews)" next to the book listing.
- **No "as seen in" or press mentions.** If anyone has written about
  you, linked to you, or mentioned you, surface it.

## 4. Confusion and Friction

### Distracting:
- **7 books listed, 5 of which are word search/puzzle books.** These
  dilute the aviation expertise positioning. A visitor scanning the
  page sees "Bugs Word Search" next to "Commercial Pilot" and the
  credibility math gets confused. The puzzle books should be on
  Amazon only, not on your professional homepage.
- **Too many storefronts.** Amazon, Gumroad, Payhip, ClawHub, App
  Store. A visitor has to figure out which store to use for which
  product. Consolidate where possible, or at least don't list every
  store on every product.

### Weak:
- **The blog index has no indication of which posts are most important.**
  13 posts listed chronologically. The Kalshi bot post and the Playbook
  post are your two strongest pieces of content and they sit in the
  same undifferentiated list as everything else.
- **"Launching Soon" on the OpenClaw Setup Service.** Either put a
  waitlist email capture on it or remove it. "Launching Soon" with
  no way to act is dead space.

### Buried:
- **The Playbook page should be a top-level nav item.** It's your
  highest-margin product ($99) and it's only accessible via the blog.
  It should be in the nav bar, not hidden behind Blog > scroll >
  find it.
- **The Flight Funded tracker** gets hero placement but the Playbook
  doesn't. The tracker is interesting; the Playbook makes money.

### Likely to cause bounce:
- **The homepage length.** Flight credentials, aircraft types, work
  history, education, coaching experience. This is a resume, not a
  landing page. Most visitors are not airline hiring managers. For
  everyone else, the bottom 60% of the page is noise.
- **No clear "I am here for X" pathways.** A student pilot, a potential
  Playbook buyer, and a tech-curious reader all get the same
  experience. One entrance, one path, one wall of everything.

## 5. Fast Fixes — Ranked Top 5

### 1. Move the Playbook to the top nav and above the fold

Add "Playbook" or "OpenClaw Playbook" as a top-level nav item. On the
homepage, move it from project #8 to project #1 or #2, right after
FeedFare. Better yet, give it a dedicated callout box on the homepage
with the stats bar (47 crons, $30/mo, $352 revenue, 0 code) and a
direct Gumroad link. This is your $99 product sitting below word
search books.

**Why first:** This is the highest revenue-per-visitor change on the
list and takes 15 minutes.

### 2. Add the Playbook CTA to the Kalshi bot post

The Kalshi post is your most compelling content. Someone who reads the
whole thing and thinks "I want to build something like this" currently
gets pointed to... your PPL Workbook. Add a callout box after the
"What's Next" section: "The full bot setup, configs, and cron jobs are
in The OpenClaw Playbook. [Get it on Gumroad — $99]". Cross-link every
technical blog post to the Playbook.

**Why second:** The Kalshi post is likely your highest-traffic blog
page and it currently has zero conversion path to your best product.

### 3. Add 2-3 interior screenshots and a buyer count to the Playbook page

Show what's inside. A SOUL.md template page, a cron job configuration
page, and the Kalshi bot architecture page would be ideal. Add "X
copies sold" if the number isn't embarrassing (even "20+ builders"
works). If you have any buyer feedback, quote it. A $99 digital
product with no preview and no social proof is a hard sell.

**Why third:** Reduces the biggest objection to purchase ("what am I
actually getting?").

### 4. Remove puzzle/word search books from the homepage

Keep the PPL Workbook, IR Workbook, and Oral Exam Flashcards. Remove
Bugs Word Search, Alaska Word Search, Army Word Search from the main
page. They're fine products but they confuse your positioning. Link
to your Amazon Author Page for the full catalog. The homepage should
show aviation expertise and tech credibility, not puzzle books.

**Why fourth:** Reduces noise and tightens the "pilot + builder"
positioning that makes the Playbook and study guides credible.

### 5. Add a "Start Here" section with 2-3 audience paths

Replace or supplement the hero buttons with clear paths:

- **Hiring a pilot?** → Flight credentials + resume download
- **Want to automate with AI?** → OpenClaw Playbook + blog posts
- **Studying for your PPL/IR?** → Aviation resources + free cheat sheets

This takes the single-path-for-everyone problem and gives each
visitor a clear next step in under 5 seconds.

**Why fifth:** Bigger lift than the others but addresses the root
cause of the "doing too many jobs" problem.
